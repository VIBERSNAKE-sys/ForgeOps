## When This File Applies

Read this file when generating cover letters, interview prep materials, or any
candidate-facing narrative content (application essays, LinkedIn messages, etc.).

## CL-GATE 0: VOICE & CONVICTION CHECK (run FIRST)

A cover letter that follows all writing rules but lacks voice will read as mechanically correct and humanly empty.

**CHECK A — POINT OF VIEW TEST:**
Does the opening express a belief, observation, or conviction? Or does it cite a company fact and state interest?
Test: Remove the company name. Does the opening still express a point of view? If YES, PASS. If it collapses to nothing, FAIL.

**CHECK B — INSIDE-OUT TEST:**
Is each paragraph written from inside the experience, or narrated from outside?
FAIL indicators: Stage directions ("He cleared his calendar"), evidence assembly ("I achieved X. I also achieved Y."), explanatory bridges ("That story matters here because...").
PASS indicators: Direct statements ("We talked for four hours"), stance declarations ("I didn't position myself as an AI person. I just was one.").

**CHECK C — DEAD SENTENCE TEST:**
Could any sentence be removed without losing information or momentum? If a paragraph has 2+ dead sentences, cut them.

If any check fails, rewrite the affected paragraph from scratch. Do not patch individual sentences — the outside-in pattern is structural.

## Writing Quality Rules (ALL narrative output)

### Banned Constructions

| Banned Pattern | Why It Fails | Write Instead |
|---------------|-------------|---------------|
| "This experience demonstrated that..." | Tells reader what to think | End on the result. Let them conclude. |
| "This taught me that..." | Same problem | Cut it. The lesson is obvious. |
| "...which resulted in..." | Passive, wordy | State the result directly. |
| "I was able to successfully..." | Hedging + redundant | "I [verb]..." |
| "During a routine [X], I mentioned..." | Windup before the pitch | Lead with the interesting part. |
| "...requirements translating into..." | Nominalization kills momentum | Use active verbs |
| One sentence doing too much | Overloaded sentences lose impact | Break it up. Short sentences hit harder. |

**Em dashes, en dashes, and double hyphens as sentence breaks are banned.** Replace with comma, colon, period, or restructure. (Inherited from resume gates.)

### Writing Principles

1. **Lead with action, not setup.**
   - BAD: "During a routine DOJ team call, I mentioned my Georgetown graduate specialization in emerging technology and AI."
   - GOOD: "I mentioned my Georgetown AI coursework on a team call, offhand, not expecting much."

2. **Cut the windup. Punch lands faster.**
   - BAD: "He invited me to what he thought would be a brief consultation, but within 20 minutes he was so engaged that he cleared my schedule for the rest of the day."
   - GOOD: "Twenty minutes in, he cleared my calendar for the day."

3. **End on results, not lessons.**
   - BAD: "The CTO invited me to join the team. This experience demonstrated that technical program management requires translating complex concepts for diverse stakeholders."
   - GOOD: "The CTO invited me to join the team."

4. **Vary sentence length and structure.**
   - Short sentences hit harder after long ones.
   - A three-word sentence. It lands.
   - **Cadence test:** If 3+ consecutive sentences are within 5 words of each other in length, the paragraph has monotone rhythm. Also: if 3+ sentences start with "I [verb]," restructure at least one.

5. **Specifics beat generics.**
   - BAD: "quickly" -> GOOD: "in 20 minutes"
   - BAD: "significant improvement" -> GOOD: "40% reduction"

6. **STAR is a skeleton, not a straitjacket.**
   - Follow the story's natural shape
   - Don't pad sections to hit arbitrary sentence counts

### Self-Check Before Finalizing

If 2+ of these are present in any paragraph, rewrite:
- Sentence starts with "This experience demonstrated..." or "This taught me..."
- Paragraph ends with a lesson/takeaway instead of a result
- No specific numbers, names, or timeframes
- Content could apply to any candidate at any company
- Passive voice dominates (>50% of sentences)
- Average sentence length > 25 words

## Cover Letter Structure

### Company Research (required before drafting)

Search for 2-3 specific, recent company facts that pass:
- **Recency:** Within 12 months preferred
- **Specificity:** Would be FALSE for their main competitor
- **Relevance:** Connects to the role being applied for

### Cover Letter Format

Use the same visual design as the CV (same fonts, header, gradient).

**Structure:**
1. **Opening paragraph:** Exit narrative adapted to this role. Why you + why this company + why now. No generic "I am writing to express my interest." Lead with the interesting part.

2. **Match table:** 4-6 rows mapping JD requirements to specific evidence. Format:

   | Your Requirement | My Evidence |
   |-----------------|-------------|
   | (from JD) | (from Evidence Ledger — specific metrics and facts) |

3. **Differentiator paragraph:** What makes Connor different from other candidates for THIS specific role. Usually: "I don't just [manage/advise/govern] — I [build/deploy/create]."

4. **Closing:** Availability, location, invitation to discuss. No "I look forward to hearing from you."

**Length:** 1 page maximum. Match table counts toward this.

## Interview Story Structure (STAR+R)

For each story, provide:

| Element | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **S** (Situation) | Context in 1-2 sentences. Lead with the interesting part. |
| **T** (Task) | What needed to happen. 1 sentence. |
| **A** (Action) | What YOU did. Be specific. 2-4 sentences. |
| **R** (Result) | Quantified outcome. 1-2 sentences. End here if the result speaks for itself. |
| **Reflection** | What was learned or what would be done differently. Signals seniority. 1 sentence. |

**Story selection by archetype (from _profile.md):**
- TPM/Program Manager: emphasize cross-functional delivery, timeline management, stakeholder alignment
- AI Governance: emphasize risk identification, framework development, policy operationalization
- AI Solutions/Deployment: emphasize customer-facing delivery, adoption, change management, technical translation
- Trust & Safety: emphasize risk categories, controls design, compliance operationalization

### Red Flag Response Scripts

Prepare responses for predictable concerns:

| Red Flag | Script |
|----------|--------|
| "Why did you leave DOJ?" | "Got caught up in DOGE layoffs occurring at that time." |
| "You don't have a CS degree" | "My path was governance-first — Georgetown Security Studies gave me the risk framework, and I built technical depth independently through DocForge, MetricsFlow, and ResForge." |
| "You can't code" | "I design systems and specify requirements — DocForge's 10-agent architecture was my design, and I built MetricsFlow's evaluation harness. I work with engineers, not instead of them." |
| "Your governance experience is one project" | "One enterprise project (53K org, 33 specialists, NIST/ISO) plus three independent AI systems I built to understand the technology I'm governing." |
| "No SaaS/private sector experience" | "My stakeholders were international law enforcement officials and IMF executives — high-stakes, complex needs. The domain changes; the delivery muscle is identical." |
